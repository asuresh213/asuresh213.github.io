Hi! I’m Arun, a math PhD student at University of Missouri. I got my B.S and M.S. in math (it was a dual degree program) from Georgia State University, where I mainly focused on commutative algebra (My master’s thesis was concerned with finding the Betti numbers of a certain numerical semigroup ring). I am originally from Chennai, a coastal city in South India, and I moved to the U.S. in the fall of 2016. In my time at GSU, Besides academics and research, I served as the President of its Mathematics and Statistics club, and the founder (and president) of The Continuum Group – a sister organization to MathStat club, dedicated towards competitive problem solving, aimed towards creating GSU’s first ever Putnam team. The group represented GSU in it’s first ever Putnam competition in 2019.

This blog was an impulsive reaction to this stunning blog of a user that I just happened to come across in MSE one fine day. So I am not quite sure where this is headed; and there certainly isn’t any grand plan (although I am quite enjoying doing this!). I want to use this blog as a place to record my thoughts, and talk about the cool math that I find online, or in general read about over the course of my PhD program.

In my personal experience, it has always been true that the more I teach and express my thoughts; the better my understanding of the subject becomes. Often times, at a PhD level, especially with self teaching various subjects, one is unable to find a whole lot of other people to torment (either because they are in the same class as you – and thus just as lost, or they have their plates full; or they just know the subject already). It is also the case that a lot of graduate level textbooks are quite dense and cryptic in writing; especially if one is self-teaching without dedicated guidance of a class or a mentor. This is true especially if the subjects in question are highly abstract and require an extensive background in allied subjects to begin with.

On more philosophical terms, there is also a fundamental disconnect in contemporary society and its view towards pure mathematics being “recreational” or “without application” (at least, anecdotally). The following quote by Henri Poincare captures really what I feel about this

The mathematician does not study pure mathematics because it is useful; he studies it because he delights in it and he delights in it because it is beautiful

This blog is my way of seeking some compromise in all of those issues. In trying to understand tricky (and important) theorems or ideas, I will try and make a post about it (and I will try to stay as close to the ground with it as possible) so that future students could read it and gain something out of it. In doing so, (if I dare say) I would like to inspire some appreciation for pure mathematics, and of the beauty that lies at the heart of an elegant argument.
